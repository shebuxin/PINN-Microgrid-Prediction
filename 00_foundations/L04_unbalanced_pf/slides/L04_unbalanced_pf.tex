\end{tikzpicture}
\vspace{0.4em}
\begin{columns}[T]
\column{0.5\textwidth}
\textbf{Physical sources}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Single-phase residential loads
  \item Single-phase rooftop PV
  \item Single-phase EV chargers
  \item Untransposed LV lines
  \item Asymmetric line geometry
\end{itemize}
\column{0.5\textwidth}
\textbf{Why these are unavoidable on LV}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Most homes have a single-phase service drop
  \item Inverters are wired to the customer's phase
  \item Transposition is rarely cost-effective at LV
  \item Loads come and go independently per phase
\end{itemize}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{What unbalance does in practice}
\begin{columns}[T]
\column{0.5\textwidth}
\textbf{Per-phase quantities decouple}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Phase A can be under-voltage while phases B and C are fine.
  \item One phase of a line can overload while average loading is below 100\%.
  \item Phase angles are no longer exactly $120^\circ$ apart.
\end{itemize}
\column{0.5\textwidth}
\textbf{System-level consequences}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Neutral conductor carries current (was zero under balance).
  \item Increased line and transformer losses.
  \item Three-phase induction motor heating and torque pulsation.
  \item Single-phase protection sees different fault levels per phase.
\end{itemize}
\end{columns}
\vspace{0.6em}
\begin{alertblock}{In this course}
The microgrid security model must \emph{see} these per-phase effects. A balanced model would silently report ``safe'' when phase A is at 0.90 pu. That is exactly the failure mode L04 fixes.
\end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

\section*{2. Symmetrical components}

\begin{frame}{The big idea: change of basis}
We have three coupled phase quantities $(\mathbf{V}_a, \mathbf{V}_b, \mathbf{V}_c)$ that interact through line and load couplings. The math is tedious.
\vspace{0.4em}

\textbf{Symmetrical components} change basis to three new quantities $(\mathbf{V}^{(0)}, \mathbf{V}^{(1)}, \mathbf{V}^{(2)})$ that, under a mild symmetry assumption on the \emph{network} (not the loads or sources), are \emph{decoupled} from each other.
\vspace{0.4em}

\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[node distance=0.5cm and 0.8cm, every node/.style={font=\scriptsize}]
\tikzset{old/.style={draw, rounded corners, fill=red!10, align=center, minimum width=2.7cm, minimum height=0.7cm}}
\tikzset{new/.style={draw, rounded corners, fill=green!12, align=center, minimum width=2.7cm, minimum height=0.7cm}}
\node[old] (p) {Phase domain\\$\mathbf{V}_a, \mathbf{V}_b, \mathbf{V}_c$\\(coupled)};
\node[new, right=of p, xshift=1cm] (s) {Sequence domain\\$\mathbf{V}^{(0)}, \mathbf{V}^{(1)}, \mathbf{V}^{(2)}$\\(decoupled)};
\draw[->, very thick] (p) -- node[above, font=\scriptsize]{Fortescue $A$} (s);
\draw[->, very thick, dashed] (s.south) to[bend left=20] node[below, font=\scriptsize]{inverse $A^{-1}$} (p.south);
\end{tikzpicture}
\vspace{0.4em}
\begin{block}{Why this is worth doing}
Solve three small \emph{decoupled} problems instead of one big coupled one. Then transform the answer back. This is the workflow inside every three-phase PF solver, including pandapower's \code{runpp\_3ph}.
\end{block}
\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{The Fortescue transformation}
With $a = e^{j 2\pi/3} = -\tfrac{1}{2} + j\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$:
\begin{equation*}
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{V}^{(0)} \\ \mathbf{V}^{(1)} \\ \mathbf{V}^{(2)} \end{bmatrix}
\;=\;
\underbrace{\frac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1   & 1   \\
1 & a   & a^2 \\
1 & a^2 & a
\end{bmatrix}}_{A^{-1}}
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{V}_a \\ \mathbf{V}_b \\ \mathbf{V}_c \end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{V}_a \\ \mathbf{V}_b \\ \mathbf{V}_c \end{bmatrix}
\;=\;
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1   & 1   \\
1 & a^2 & a   \\
1 & a   & a^2
\end{bmatrix}}_{A}
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{V}^{(0)} \\ \mathbf{V}^{(1)} \\ \mathbf{V}^{(2)} \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\mathbf{V}^{(0)}$ -- \textbf{zero sequence}: the common-mode part. All three phases shift in unison.
  \item $\mathbf{V}^{(1)}$ -- \textbf{positive sequence}: the balanced rotating ABC part. ``True'' balanced operation.
  \item $\mathbf{V}^{(2)}$ -- \textbf{negative sequence}: balanced rotating ACB (opposite). A pure unbalance signal.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{alertblock}{The same transform for currents}
$(\mathbf{I}_a, \mathbf{I}_b, \mathbf{I}_c) \leftrightarrow (\mathbf{I}^{(0)}, \mathbf{I}^{(1)}, \mathbf{I}^{(2)})$ with the same matrix. We will apply it to load currents shortly.
\end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Sequence components of three common patterns}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Pattern (phase domain) & $\mathbf{V}_a$ & $\mathbf{V}_b$ & $\mathbf{V}_c$ & Sequence content \\
\midrule
Perfectly balanced & $V$ & $a^2 V$ & $a V$ & $\mathbf{V}^{(1)} = V$ only \\
All-A only & $V$ & 0 & 0 & $\mathbf{V}^{(0)}\!=\!\mathbf{V}^{(1)}\!=\!\mathbf{V}^{(2)}\!=\!V/3$ \\
Two phases ($V_a\!=\!V_b\!=\!V, V_c\!=\!0$) & $V$ & $V$ & 0 & non-trivial $V^{(0,1,2)}$ \\
Pure unbalance ($V$, $a V$, $a^2 V$) & $V$ & $a V$ & $a^2 V$ & $\mathbf{V}^{(2)} = V$ only \\
Zero-sequence only & $V$ & $V$ & $V$ & $\mathbf{V}^{(0)} = V$ only \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\vspace{0.6em}
\begin{block}{The takeaway}
Single-phase load $\rightarrow$ \emph{equal} positive, negative, and zero sequence parts. This is exactly why a single-phase load excites \emph{all three} sequence networks, and why the unbalanced response can be large.
\end{block}
\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Sequence impedances: where they come from}
\centering
\scriptsize
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{0.24\textwidth}p{0.24\textwidth}p{0.44\textwidth}@{}}
\toprule
Element & Typical $Z^{(0)}$ vs $Z^{(1)}$ & Why \\
\midrule
Transmission line, transposed & $Z^{(0)} \approx 3\text{--}5 \cdot Z^{(1)}$ & ground/earth return path is high-impedance \\
LV cable, 4-wire & $Z^{(0)} \approx 2\text{--}4 \cdot Z^{(1)}$ & neutral conductor + ground return \\
LV cable, 3-wire (no neutral) & $Z^{(0)}$ very large or undefined & no zero-sequence path \\
Synchronous generator & machine-specific & neutral grounding and winding data matter \\
Grounded-wye transformer side & finite if a return path exists & vector group and grounding define the path \\
Delta winding & blocks zero-sequence line current across that side & zero-sequence current may circulate inside the delta \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\vspace{0.6em}
\begin{alertblock}{Practical rule}
$Z^{(0)}$ is usually \emph{larger} than $Z^{(1)}$ because zero-sequence current returns through the neutral or earth. That is also why phase-to-ground faults produce different fault currents than three-phase faults. We will use $Z^{(0)} = 2\,Z^{(1)}$ in our worked example.
\end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

\node[bus] (aR) at (4, 1.0) {};
\node[bus] (bR) at (4, 0.5) {};
\node[bus] (cR) at (4, 0.0) {};
\node[bus] (nR) at (4,-0.6) {};
\draw[thick, phasea] (a)--(aR) node[midway, above, font=\tiny]{$I_a$};
\draw[thick, phaseb] (b)--(bR) node[midway, above, font=\tiny]{$I_b$};
\draw[thick, phasec] (c)--(cR) node[midway, above, font=\tiny]{$I_c$};
\draw[thick, dashed] (n)--(nR) node[midway, below, font=\tiny]{$I_n = I_a + I_b + I_c = 3 I^{(0)}$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\vspace{0.5em}
\begin{columns}[T]
\column{0.55\textwidth}
\textbf{Two equivalent statements}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Under balance, $I_a + I_b + I_c = 0$. No neutral current.
  \item In sequence terms, $I^{(0)} = (I_a+I_b+I_c)/3 = 0$.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Under unbalance,} the neutral carries $3\,I^{(0)}$. If the neutral is absent or undersized, the zero-sequence current has nowhere to go and unbalance becomes very expensive.
\column{0.42\textwidth}
\begin{block}{Why microgrids care}
LV microgrid feeders often have a neutral. Single-phase loads cause $I^{(0)} \neq 0$, neutral conductors heat up, and zero-sequence voltage drift can affect every customer on the feeder.
\end{block}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\section*{4. Three-phase PF formulations}

\begin{frame}{Phase domain vs sequence domain}
\centering
\scriptsize
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{0.28\textwidth}p{0.30\textwidth}p{0.32\textwidth}@{}}
\toprule
 & \textbf{Phase-domain PF} & \textbf{Sequence-domain PF} \\
\midrule
State variables & $V_a, V_b, V_c$ at each bus & $V^{(0)}, V^{(1)}, V^{(2)}$ at each bus \\
Network coupling & lines have full $3\times 3$ impedance matrices & three decoupled networks \\
Loads / DER & native phase-domain representation & must transform between domains every iteration \\
Best for & arbitrary line geometry, untransposed lines & symmetric line geometry, balanced topology with unbalanced injection \\
Common in & Kersting, OpenDSS & pandapower, fault studies, Grainger \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\vspace{0.6em}
\begin{block}{Choosing}
If your lines are nicely symmetric (transposed or short LV with similar phase impedances), sequence-domain is cleaner. If geometry is genuinely asymmetric, phase-domain avoids approximation. \textbf{This course uses pandapower, so we work in sequence domain.}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{pandapower's hybrid approach}
\code{pp.runpp\_3ph} solves the three sequence networks at every iteration, with a small twist:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Positive network} -- solved by full Newton-Raphson. This is the only network with a balanced slack source (the external grid) and PV-type generators.
  \item \textbf{Negative network} -- solved by current injection. No internal sources; everything is driven by load and DER unbalance currents.
  \item \textbf{Zero network} -- solved by current injection. Same logic, but with the appropriate $Z^{(0)}$ and grounding topology.
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{0.4em}
\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[node distance=0.5cm and 1.0cm, every node/.style={font=\scriptsize}]
\tikzset{box/.style={draw, rounded corners, align=center, minimum width=2.5cm, minimum height=0.7cm}}
\tikzset{arr/.style={-{Latex[length=2mm]}, thick}}
\node[box, fill=red!12] (phase) {Phase domain\\$\mathbf{S}_a, \mathbf{S}_b, \mathbf{S}_c$};
\node[box, fill=blue!12, right=of phase] (seq) {Sequence domain\\NR + current injection};
\node[box, fill=green!12, right=of seq] (back) {Phase domain\\$\mathbf{V}_a, \mathbf{V}_b, \mathbf{V}_c$};
\draw[arr] (phase) -- node[above, font=\tiny]{$A^{-1}$} (seq);
\draw[arr] (seq) -- node[above, font=\tiny]{$A$} (back);
\end{tikzpicture}
\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{block}{What you see as a user}
Just call \code{pp.runpp\_3ph(net)}. The transformations and the three-network solve happen inside.
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\section*{5. Fully worked example}

\begin{frame}{Setup: 2-bus LV feeder with a single-phase load}
\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[node distance=2.0cm and 2.5cm, every node/.style={font=\scriptsize}]
\tikzset{bus/.style={draw, circle, minimum size=0.7cm, fill=blue!10}}
\tikzset{ld/.style={draw, rounded corners, fill=phasea!30, minimum width=1.7cm, minimum height=0.6cm}}
\node[bus] (b1) {1};
\node[bus, right=of b1] (b2) {2};
\node[font=\scriptsize, above=0.2cm of b1] {balanced slack, $V_1 = 1.00 \angle 0^\circ$};
\node[ld, below=0.4cm of b2] {$S_a = 0.4 + j0$, $S_b = S_c = 0$};
\draw[thick] (b1) -- node[above, font=\scriptsize]{$Z^{(1)}\!=\!Z^{(2)}\!=\!0.05\!+\!j0.05$, $Z^{(0)}\!=\!0.10\!+\!j0.10$} (b2);
\draw (b2)--++(0,-0.4);
\end{tikzpicture}
\vspace{0.4em}
\textbf{All values in per-unit on a 1 MVA / 0.4 kV base.}\\[0.2em]
The load is on phase A only; phases B and C are unloaded. The slack at bus 1 produces a perfectly balanced source. We are about to compute the voltages at bus 2 by hand, then verify with pandapower.
\vspace{0.4em}
\begin{block}{Strategy}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Phase currents at bus 2 $\to$ sequence currents (Fortescue).
  \item Sequence voltage drops $\to$ sequence voltages at bus 2.
  \item Sequence voltages $\to$ phase voltages (inverse Fortescue).
  \item Read off $|V_a|, |V_b|, |V_c|$ and compute VUF.
\end{enumerate}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}[shrink=5]{Step 1 -- Phase currents to sequence currents}
\textbf{Flat-start linear approximation:} assume $V_2 \approx 1$ so that $I_a = (S_a/V_a)^* \approx S_a$. (The constant-power feedback as $V$ drops is left to the iterative solver; we revisit in Step 4.)
\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{equation*}
I_a \approx 0.4, \qquad I_b = 0, \qquad I_c = 0.
\end{equation*}
Apply $A^{-1}$:
\begin{align*}
I^{(0)} &= \tfrac{1}{3}(I_a + I_b + I_c) = \tfrac{1}{3}(0.4) = 0.1333 \\
I^{(1)} &= \tfrac{1}{3}(I_a + a\,I_b + a^2\,I_c) = \tfrac{1}{3}(0.4) = 0.1333 \\
I^{(2)} &= \tfrac{1}{3}(I_a + a^2\,I_b + a\,I_c) = \tfrac{1}{3}(0.4) = 0.1333
\end{align*}
\vspace{0.2em}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lc}
\toprule
Sequence & Current $I^{(k)}$ (pu) \\
\midrule
Zero & $0.1333$ \\
Positive & $0.1333$ \\
Negative & $0.1333$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\vspace{0.4em}
\begin{block}{Observation}
A single-phase load excites \emph{all three} sequence networks \emph{equally}. This is why ``single phase'' is the worst case for unbalance: it produces the maximum amount of negative and zero sequence current per unit of phase current.
\end{block}
\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Step 2 -- Sequence voltage drops at bus 2}
Each sequence current flows through its own sequence impedance from slack to bus 2:
\begin{align*}
V^{(1)}_2 &= V^{(1)}_1 - Z^{(1)} \, I^{(1)} = 1.00 - (0.05 + j0.05)(0.1333) = 0.9933 - j0.0067 \\
V^{(2)}_2 &= 0 \;-\; Z^{(2)} \, I^{(2)} = -(0.05 + j0.05)(0.1333) = -0.0067 - j0.0067 \\
V^{(0)}_2 &= 0 \;-\; Z^{(0)} \, I^{(0)} = -(0.10 + j0.10)(0.1333) = -0.0133 - j0.0133
\end{align*}
\vspace{0.3em}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Sequence & $V^{(k)}_2$ (complex pu) & $|V^{(k)}_2|$ \\
\midrule
Zero & $-0.0133 - j0.0133$ & 0.0189 \\
Positive & $\phantom{-}0.9933 - j0.0067$ & 0.9934 \\
Negative & $-0.0067 - j0.0067$ & 0.0094 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\vspace{0.4em}
\begin{alertblock}{Sanity}
Positive-sequence voltage at bus 2 is $0.993$ pu -- which would be the answer if this were a balanced $0.133$ pu per-phase load. The negative and zero sequences are the ``cost'' of moving all of it onto phase A.
\end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}[shrink=4]{Step 3 -- Sequence voltages to phase voltages (linear estimate)}
Apply $A$ (inverse Fortescue):
\begin{align*}
\mathbf{V}_a &= V^{(0)} + V^{(1)} + V^{(2)} \\
             &= (-0.0133 - j0.0133) + (0.9933 - j0.0067) + (-0.0067 - j0.0067) = 0.9733 - j0.0267 \\
\mathbf{V}_b &= V^{(0)} + a^2 V^{(1)} + a V^{(2)} = -0.5066 - j0.8726 \\
\mathbf{V}_c &= V^{(0)} + a V^{(1)} + a^2 V^{(2)} = -0.5067 + j0.8594
\end{align*}
\vspace{0.3em}
Magnitudes:
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Phase & $\mathbf{V}_\phi$ (complex pu) & $|\mathbf{V}_\phi|$ \\
\midrule
A & $\phantom{-}0.9733 - j0.0267$ & \textbf{0.974} \\
B & $-0.5066 - j0.8726$ & \textbf{1.009} \\
C & $-0.5067 + j0.8594$ & \textbf{0.998} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\vspace{0.4em}
\begin{alertblock}{What the linear estimate is telling us}
At this stage hand math says: phase A drops to $0.974$, phase B rises slightly to $1.009$, phase C stays near nominal. \emph{No phase voltage crosses the $[0.95, 1.05]$ band.} VUF (computed shortly) will also look modest.
\\[0.3em]
\textbf{But this is the flat-start, first-order answer.} Real loads are constant-power, so as $|V_a|$ drops, the actual current grows. We verify on the next slide.
\end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}[shrink=8]{Step 4 -- Verify with pandapower (constant-power, iterated)}
Running \code{pp.runpp\_3ph} on the same network with the load modeled as constant-power (the default) and iterating to convergence:
\vspace{0.4em}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
 & $|V_a|$ & $|V_b|$ & $|V_c|$ & VUF \\
\midrule
Hand math (Step 3, linear flat-start) & 0.974 & 1.009 & 0.998 & 0.95\% \\
\code{pp.runpp\_3ph} (converged) & \textbf{0.908} & \textbf{1.029} & 0.995 & \textbf{3.19\%} \\
$\Delta$ (truth $-$ linear estimate) & $-0.066$ & $+0.020$ & $-0.003$ & $+2.24\%$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\vspace{0.6em}
\begin{block}{Why the linear estimate under-reports}
As $|V_a|$ drops, a constant-power load draws \emph{more} current ($I = (S/V)^*$). More current produces more voltage drop on all excited sequence networks, which feeds back into a larger $V$ drop. In this stressed example, the iterative solution therefore differs materially from the flat-start estimate.
\end{block}
\begin{alertblock}{Take-away}
Linear hand math gives intuition. \textbf{For safety screening, always use the iterative solver.} The project's Week 4 labeling code is exactly this: \code{runpp\_3ph} on every scenario $\times$ contingency.
\end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

\section*{6. Voltage Unbalance Factor}

\begin{frame}[shrink=3]{VUF and a common magnitude-deviation metric}
\begin{block}{IEC 61000-4-30 (true VUF)}
\begin{equation*}
\text{VUF} \;=\; \frac{|\mathbf{V}^{(2)}|}{|\mathbf{V}^{(1)}|} \times 100\%
\end{equation*}
The ratio of negative-sequence to positive-sequence voltage magnitude.
\end{block}
\begin{block}{NEMA MG 1 percent voltage unbalance (not the same metric)}
\begin{equation*}
\%\mathrm{VU}_{\mathrm{NEMA}}
=100\,\frac{\max\limits_{xy\in\{ab,bc,ca\}}\bigl||V_{xy}|-\bar V_{LL}\bigr|}{\bar V_{LL}},
\quad \bar V_{LL}=\tfrac{|V_{ab}|+|V_{bc}|+|V_{ca}|}{3}.
\end{equation*}
It uses line-to-line voltage magnitudes; it is a useful motor-oriented proxy, but it is not algebraically equivalent to sequence VUF.
\end{block}
\vspace{0.3em}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Reference practice & Metric and teaching reference \\
\midrule
IEC 61000-2-2 (public LV compatibility) & sequence VUF: $2\%$ \\
NEMA MG 1 (motor terminals) & percent voltage unbalance: $1\%$ recommended maximum \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}[shrink=5]{VUF on our worked example}
\textbf{From hand math (Step 2):}
\begin{align*}
|\mathbf{V}^{(1)}_2| &= |0.9933 - j0.0067| \approx 0.9934 \\
|\mathbf{V}^{(2)}_2| &= |-0.0067 - j0.0067| \approx 0.0094
\end{align*}
\begin{equation*}
\text{VUF}_{\text{linear}} \;=\; \frac{0.0094}{0.9934} \times 100\% \;\approx\; \mathbf{0.95\%} \quad (\text{below 2\% limit -- no violation in linear estimate})
\end{equation*}
\vspace{0.2em}
\textbf{From pandapower (Step 4):}
\begin{equation*}
\boxed{\;\text{VUF}_{\text{converged}} \;\approx\; \mathbf{3.19\%}\;}
\end{equation*}
This is above both the IEC 2\% limit \emph{and} the ANSI 3\% limit. The converged state has two simultaneous violations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Phase-A undervoltage ($|V_a| = 0.908 < 0.95$).
  \item VUF excursion ($3.19\% > 2\%$).
\end{itemize}
\vspace{0.2em}
\begin{alertblock}{Why the project tracks VUF separately}
Even when no individual phase voltage crosses its limit, a high VUF damages three-phase motors and trips sensitive equipment. The Week 4 labeling code treats VUF as a stand-alone violation type, computed from \code{res\_bus\_3ph.unbalance\_percent}.
\end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

\section*{7. Modeling unbalanced systems in pandapower}

\begin{frame}[fragile,shrink=8]{Asymmetric loads and sources}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
import pandapower as pp

net = pp.create_empty_network(sn_mva=1.0)
b1 = pp.create_bus(net, vn_kv=0.4)
b2 = pp.create_bus(net, vn_kv=0.4)
pp.create_ext_grid(net, bus=b1, vm_pu=1.0,
    s_sc_max_mva=1e6, rx_max=0.1,
    x0x_max=1.0, r0x0_max=0.1)
pp.create_line_from_parameters(net, b1, b2, length_km=1,
    r_ohm_per_km=0.008, x_ohm_per_km=0.008, c_nf_per_km=0.0,
    r0_ohm_per_km=0.016, x0_ohm_per_km=0.016, c0_nf_per_km=0.0,
    max_i_ka=10.0)

# Single-phase load: 0.4 pu on phase A, zero on B and C (1 MVA base)
pp.create_asymmetric_load(net, bus=b2,
    p_a_mw=0.4, q_a_mvar=0.0,
    p_b_mw=0.0, q_b_mvar=0.0,
    p_c_mw=0.0, q_c_mvar=0.0,
    type="wye")
\end{lstlisting}
\begin{block}{Key new parameters}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \code{r0\_ohm\_per\_km}, \code{x0\_ohm\_per\_km}: zero-sequence line parameters.
  \item \code{create\_asymmetric\_load}: explicit per-phase $P, Q$, plus wye/delta type.
  \item \code{create\_asymmetric\_sgen}: same idea for PV or BESS on one phase.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\section*{8. The five violation types}

\begin{frame}{What the project labels as a ``violation''}
The supervised dataset built in Week 4 of the project assigns a binary label \texttt{violation} that is the logical OR of five sub-flags:
\vspace{0.3em}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\# & Violation type & Detected from \\
\midrule
1 & Phase under- or over-voltage & \code{vm\_a\_pu}, \code{vm\_b\_pu}, \code{vm\_c\_pu} vs $[0.95, 1.05]$ \\
2 & Line / transformer phase overload & \code{loading\_a/b/c\_percent} vs 100\% \\
3 & Voltage unbalance factor & \code{unbalance\_percent} vs 2\% \\
4 & Service loss & topology check: are loads still connected? \\
5 & Non-convergence & \code{net.converged\_3ph} flag \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\vspace{0.6em}
\begin{block}{The pattern}
The first three are read straight out of \code{runpp\_3ph}'s result tables. The fourth is a graph reachability check on the post-contingency network. The fifth is the solver's own status flag.
\end{block}
\end{frame}
